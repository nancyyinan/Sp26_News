Full Reflection

The Essay

"Share a Story made my news habits visible in a way that normal scrolling does not."

01Seeing My News Habits as Data

Over the semester, Share a Story made my news habits visible in a way that normal scrolling does not. Before looking at my data, I thought of my news choices as mostly practical: I saw a headline, judged whether it seemed important, and decided whether to share it. But after downloading my own data and comparing it with my section's data, I realized that my choices formed a clear pattern. I was not just reading "the news" in a general sense. I was repeatedly choosing stories about global systems: geopolitics, markets, technology, war, AI, trade, energy, and institutional power. Share a Story helped me see both the strength and the limitation of that habit.

02A Concentrated News Diet

The clearest pattern in my own data is source concentration. Out of 76 stories I shared, 38 came from the Financial Times, which is exactly 50 percent of my submissions. Another 24 came from The New York Times, about 31.6 percent. Together, these two sources made up more than 80 percent of my news diet.

That surprised me because I did not realize how narrow my source range was until I saw it as data. I usually think of myself as reading different stories, but in reality many of those stories came through the same two institutional frames.

The Financial Times especially shaped how I understood news because it often connects events to markets, policy decisions, business strategy, and global risk. This made my summaries more analytical, but it also limited the kinds of perspectives I encountered.

The section data made this pattern even clearer. Across the class, there were 1,327 shared stories from a much wider range of sources. The New York Times was still the most common source in the section, but it made up only about 27.9 percent of the class submissions. The top two sources in the section made up about 37 percent, while my top two sources made up more than 80 percent. The class data included sources such as NPR, BBC, CNN, AP, NBC, The Guardian, Vogue, Variety, Rolling Stone, and Gothamist, along with stories found through Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, Reddit, Google, Apple News, and other platforms. Compared with that, my own data shows a much more controlled and less casual way of finding news. I was mostly going directly to publications I already trusted.

03Reading Through Systems

This pattern says something about how I read the world. I tend to be drawn to stories where one event affects many systems at once. For example, I shared several stories about conflict and its effects on oil prices, aviation, inflation, global trade, and financial markets. I also shared stories about AI companies, software vulnerabilities, data centers, and the economic pressure created by new technology. These stories interested me because they were not only about one person, one company, or one event. They showed chain reactions. A war is not only a military story; it can become an energy story, a travel story, a market story, and a consumer story. AI is not only a technology story; it is also a labor, infrastructure, investment, and regulation story.

Because of this, Share a Story helped me practice a more structural way of reading news. The "what happened" and "why it matters" format was especially useful. It forced me to separate the basic event from its significance. A headline may tell me what happened, but the "why it matters" part asks me to explain the consequences. Over time, I became less interested in simply choosing a dramatic headline and more interested in asking: Who is affected? What system is being revealed? What institution has power here? What economic or political consequences might follow? This was probably the most valuable habit I developed through the project.

04What My Data Leaves Out

However, my data also shows what is missing from my news habits. If most of my stories come from the Financial Times and The New York Times, then my understanding of the news is shaped by elite English-language media. These sources are useful, but they do not represent every perspective. My submissions included fewer local stories, fewer community-based stories, fewer cultural stories, and fewer stories centered on ordinary people's daily experiences. I also shared less from smaller publications, nonprofit newsrooms, student journalism, or sources outside the major U.S., European, and Chinese geopolitical frame. This matters because a healthy news habit should not only mean reading serious global news. It should also mean paying attention to whose lives are made visible and whose lives are left out.

Compared with the section, I also seemed less influenced by social media discovery. This is both a strength and a weakness. On one hand, going directly to trusted publications may help me avoid misinformation, low-quality viral content, or emotionally manipulative posts. On the other hand, social platforms sometimes expose people to stories that mainstream institutional sources may not prioritize, especially local, cultural, or community-centered stories. The problem is not that one path is better than the other. The problem is that every path creates blind spots. My path gave me depth in global systems, but less variety in voice, scale, and emotional perspective.

05Feedback on Share a Story

As a learning tool, Share a Story succeeded because it made news reading active instead of passive. I was not only consuming headlines; I had to choose one story, summarize it, and explain why it mattered. My favorite part was the discipline of writing a short "why it matters" statement. It trained me to think more like an editor: What is the real significance of this story? Why should someone else care? The most difficult part was that the activity sometimes felt repetitive. Without regular feedback, it was hard to know whether my summaries were improving or whether my source choices were becoming too narrow. I would have liked more feedback on writing clarity, source reliability, source diversity, and whether my "why it matters" explanation was specific enough.

For future students, I would suggest treating Share a Story not only as a daily assignment, but as a personal news map. Students should occasionally look back at their own submissions and ask what patterns are forming. What sources do they rely on? What topics do they keep choosing? What kinds of stories do they avoid? The platform itself could support this by adding small dashboards, such as source breakdowns, topic tags, submission timelines, and comparisons with the section average. These features would make the learning goal more visible.

06Final Takeaway

Overall, Share a Story helped me build a more intentional news habit. It did not make my news diet perfectly balanced, but it helped me understand my own pattern. I learned that I am strongest at connecting individual stories to larger systems, especially in politics, economics, technology, and global risk. I also learned that I need to expand my attention toward more local, cultural, emotional, and community-based perspectives. For me, the value of Share a Story was not only that it made me read more news. It made me question how I read, what I trust, and what I might be missing.
